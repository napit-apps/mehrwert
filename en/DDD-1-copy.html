Rethinking IT Modernization – The Role of Domain-Driven Design

Marcel, 14.03.2026

IT modernization is on almost every agenda today. New market demands, increasing cost pressure, and the need for greater flexibility are driving companies to question their existing systems.

An example from a recent project shows what these situations typically look like:

A monolithic system grown over many years, built on more than 25 technologies and programming languages.
Over 200 individual installations, many with customer-specific customizations.
And a team where no one has a complete understanding of the system as a whole.

This is where many modernization initiatives begin.
And this is exactly where they often fail.

The real problem is not technology

The most common reaction is technology-driven:
a new architecture, a new platform, sometimes even a complete rewrite.

But these approaches fall short.

Without a clear understanding of the business domain, existing complexity is not reduced – it is simply reimplemented.
The result: the same problems, just on a more modern technology stack.

The key lever is not technology.
It is understanding the system.

Domain-Driven Design as a foundation

Domain-Driven Design (DDD) addresses exactly this challenge.
Instead of focusing on technical structure, it puts the business perspective at the center.

What domains exist?
How are they connected?
Which parts are truly business-critical?

Only when these questions are answered does a solid foundation for decision-making emerge.

In a recent workshop, this step became the turning point.
Instead of jumping directly into target architectures, we first built a shared understanding of the business domain.

The key difference:
not only IT was involved, but also the business stakeholders.

The key insight

One of the most important realizations was surprisingly simple:

Modernization is not an IT-only topic.

Only through collaboration across different perspectives did a shared understanding of the domains emerge.
And this understanding became the foundation for all further decisions.

Another critical aspect:
from within the organization, achieving this level of clarity is often difficult.

There are too many assumptions, too many dependencies, and too many implicit decisions.
An external perspective helps to make structures visible and enables real prioritization.

Why this matters

Without a clear domain understanding, decisions are made blindly.
With a clear domain model, direction emerges.

  • Which parts of the system actually need modernization – and which do not?
  • Where does investment truly pay off?
  • Which dependencies determine the sequence of implementation?

These are not technical questions.
They are business questions.

Outlook

Domain-Driven Design is not an end in itself.
It is a tool to understand complexity and reduce it deliberately.

The next step is to turn this understanding into concrete decisions:
migration strategies, transformation waves, and a solid roadmap.

And this is where it becomes particularly interesting – especially in combination with new technologies like LLMs.

More on that in the next post.
